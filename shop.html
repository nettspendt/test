Understanding Circles

Circles are one of the most important shapes in geometry because every point on a circle is the same distance from its center. Engineers use circles when designing gears and wheels, architects use them when creating domes, and mathematicians use them to understand relationships between distance, angles, and area. Before solving problems involving circles, it is important to understand the parts that make up a circle and how they work together.

Important Circle Vocabulary

Radius

A segment connecting the center of the circle to any point on the circle. Every radius in a circle has the same length.

Diameter

A chord that passes through the center of the circle. The diameter is always twice the radius.

Chord

A segment whose endpoints lie on the circle.

Secant

A line that intersects the circle at exactly two points.

Types of Arcs

Understanding Arcs of a Circle

Whenever we travel along the edge of a circle, we are moving along an arc. Arcs are everywhere in the real world, from the path of a Ferris wheel to the curved shape of bridges and roads. Understanding arcs allows us to determine distances around circles and understand how angles and circles are related.

Every arc has a measure expressed in degrees. The measure of an arc corresponds directly to the measure of the central angle that intercepts it. Because a complete circle contains 360°, every arc is part of that total.

Minor Arc

A minor arc measures less than 180°. It is the shortest path between two points on a circle.

Semicircle

A semicircle measures exactly 180° and is formed by a diameter.

Major Arc

A major arc measures more than 180° and travels the longer way around the circle.

Inscribed Angles

An inscribed angle has its vertex on the circle.

Inscribed Angle = Intercepted Arc ÷ 2

Example

If the intercepted arc measures 120°, the inscribed angle measures 60°.

Finding the Area of a Sector

A sector is a portion of a circle enclosed by two radii and an arc. Sectors appear everywhere in everyday life, from slices of pizza to pie charts and Ferris wheels. Since a sector is only part of a circle, its area depends on what fraction of the circle it represents.

Area of Sector = (θ ÷ 360) × πr²

Worked Example

Find the area of a sector with a central angle of 90° and radius of 10 units.

A = (90 ÷ 360)(π)(10²)
A = (1/4)(100π)
A = 25π units²

Regular Polygons

A regular polygon has equal sides and equal angles.

Area = ½aP

Example

Apothem = 8
Perimeter = 48

A = ½(8)(48) = 192 units²

Connections Between Topics

  • Circles help define regular polygons.
  • The apothem acts like a radius inside polygons.
  • Sector area uses the same area formula as circles.
  • Arc measures connect directly to angle measures.
